Tabletop Battles Reviews: Wazdakka Gutsmek Model

April 25th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Armageddon is back! Well, I guess it never went anywhere, but it's returned to the focus of the 41st millennium, and with it, a new Plastic Yarrick and a whole host of new Astra Militarum releases. But for us green-pilled, die-hard Ork players, we finally get a long-hoped-for, but never-before-made, miniature for Wazdakka Gutsmek.

Before we go too far, we’d like to thank Games Workshop for providing us with preview copies of all of these releases for review purposes.

A Quick History Lesson

DYLON: Wazdakka Gutsmek first appeared in 1994 with the release of the Ork codex for Second edition 40k. Back in the days of wargear cards not-included in the codex, hit location charts, and random shenanigans, his warbike was equipped with a Battlecannon that fired over his head (pushing the bike back D3" every time) in addition to the usual twin-linked autocannons. This armament lead to many, and varied, conversions, using all sorts of bits to represent the bike and rider. His rules include a D6 chart (gotta love 'em) that dictated how addicted he was to speed, with a possible result forcing him to go flat out, which could cause him to drive off the table! Needless to say, the times were different.



In 3rd edition, with a short story included to build his character further and with the advent of some ork-specific weaponry, the battle cannon was swapped for a Kustom Blasta, though the autocannons remained. The random movement rules returned with the possibility to drive off the table or into dangerous terrain present. Fixit makes his first appearance, assisting with difficult terrain tests.



Wazdakka's last appearance, as after this codex entries without minis were retired, was in the 4th edition book. His codex entry included a new story and much more streamlined rules, eliminating the possibility for his mid-game disappearing act, and the omission of Fixit (pour one out for the git).



A special note has to be made for all the Ork players over the years, taking their time to lovingly create conversions of Wazdakka. They highlight the ingenuity of those hobbyists trying to figure out how to create a coherent model for what at times has been a wild concept. It's always been a lot to create a suitable mini for the baddest Ork Biker.

And then nothing for more than a decade. Adherents to the Kult of Speed had to live without their favorite character, at least, until now.

Previewed at Adepticon 2026, Wazdakka returns with a massive, new plastic kit. Fixit is back too! Read our hot take on the new datasheet here.

Details

DYLON: In the box is a 120mm oval base and two sprues. You'll use everything with the only optional parts being the shockwave. There are little details hidden all over the parts of this kit, with stuff hidden deep in the finished mini. It's forward thinking though, as they could be used for conversions. Things like the back of the seatback being fully detailed, or a loose bullet sitting on top of the skidplate under the engine. I also really appreciate how the whole bike looks like it could work; brake disks and cables up front, belt drive from the engine to the rear wheel. It's like someone who has worked on motorcycles designed the kit.

Assembly

DYLON: I made the big decision before I started assembling the kit to cut Wazdakka off his very-large hero rock. With that, I dove in and within about 2 hours had the full kit assembled. It was super straight forward with the instructions even showing stopping points for Wazdakka and Fixit to leave them unglued to paint as sub-assemblies. Pretty cool to see the kit designed with that in-mind. Cutting the rock off the bike was the trickiest bit, figuring out how much of it I wanted to retain and how much of the rock I needed to glue together before I started cutting.



I should have glued on the main rock as well, which I ended up having to cut after the fact, but that wasn't a huge hurdle to get past. I trimmed the back of the rock to fit on the base and position the entire model nicely. I took all the other chunks of rock from the kit and cut them down to look like shards of shale in the ground around the mini.



The other thing to manage was the position of Wazdakka's head. As-sculpted, and with the bike in a new, more-conventional orientation, his head is looking way too far down. I took an X-acto to the neck and cut it back enough to get the head tilted back and added an off-cut of sprue to support it. I mixed up some Green Stuff (RIP) and sculpted a rudimentary new neck, which I think turned out pretty well.



Liam: I did not do any of this, because I like the pose as it is - and also it has a significant impact on the height of the model, which on baseline is comfortably taller than an Armiger. 90% of the assembly on this guy is a dream. My only notes are that the front forks can be a little tricky in that "I wish I had one more hand to hold all this in place" way, and that if you build the flames coming out of the back they initially assemble by joining four quarters of a circle together and it's finicky to get them to all sit level with each other and then glue them to the bike exhaust. Those are pretty minimal gripes for a model of this size with 50ish parts, though, and the rest is very clean indeed. As Dylan mentioned, while the bike design is obviously fantastical (most real bikes don't have jet engines), it does feel like it was sculpted by someone who has a good grip on how bikes go together.

Painting

DYLON: As I've done with all my modern Ork vehicles, I started with a coat of Rustoleum Ruddy Primer.



I painted all the armor panels of the bike with sponges. First OG Citadel Vermin Brown, then Mephiston Red, and finished with Wild Rider Red. I followed that with a heavy drybrush of Ironbreaker on all the metal areas, being sure to leave a decent amount of the rusty base showing. All the metallic parts received a wash of Nuln Oil. The tires and cables were painted with Black, and anything bone was based with Dryad Bark and highlighted to Ushabti Bone. I used XV-88 for all the leather, and all the non-metallic parts across the mini were dry brushed with Ushabti Bone. It ties it all together and adds a dusty-ness that I wanted.

Wazdakka Gutsmek. Credit: 40khamslam.

Wazdakka's skin is still the same method I’ve been using since 1999, drybrushing successive layers of Dark Angels Green, Snot Green, and Goblin Green, all from the same old hex pots I had back then. Only change is adding a final mixed highlight of Goblin Green and white to push the contrast of the skin.

Wazdakka Gutsmek. Credit: 40khamslam.

His vest was painted black and his pants with Corvus Black, and finished with a bone dry brush. I debated leaving it like this or going in with layered highlights, but I was pretty happy with it as it gave a dry, weatherbeaten feel to both materials. I painted their goggles with a traditional gem effect from Coat d'Arms Wizard Blue to White, before dotting them white and adding a layer of ‘Ard Coat.

Wazdakka Gutsmek. Credit: 40khamslam.

Wazdakka Gutsmek. Credit: 40khamslam.

Liam: My major reflection on Wazdakka is that it's a hell of a lot of model. I ended up doing it over several days and breaking it into sections for my own sanity, getting the front end done first, then moving to the body of the bike, up to Wazdakka himself, and then on to the details on the seat back and the exhaust, before tackling the flames and smoke and tidying up the basing. It doesn't initially look that intimidating, but there's tons of details on this thing that you find as you go.

Wazdakka Gutsmek. Credit: Corrode

I did my final couple of hours on him on a Sunday morning and had to convince myself to stop painting. I'm happy with my result, but I can still see where I could have done more - different metals to pop different parts of the bike, adding more gradation to the skulls and flames, picking out a couple more rivets. I'm really excited to see what competition-level painters do with this guy as I think there's tons of possibilities here to do all kinds of cool stuff, with some nice open areas to work with.

Final Thoughts, Parting Shots, and What Have You

DYLON: I'm very happy with this kit. It's easy to go together, easy enough to do a minor conversion, bits galore if you want to take it and make something else or use parts elsewhere. I think they did a great job interpreting the story and rules into the mini and making it a functional-looking bike to haul this legend around.

Liam: I love him. The model absolutely slaps, going exactly as over the top as it should do, and the rules are great too. I can't wait to get him on the table.
