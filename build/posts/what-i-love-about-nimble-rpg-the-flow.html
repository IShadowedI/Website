What I Love About Nimble RPG: The Flow

April 28th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Nimble is a relatively new TTRPG I’ve been playing now for a few years. It started back in 2023 as a short “zine” of modular rules to swap and replace a few key mechanics in D&D 5E. The success of that first Kickstarter in 2023 led to another crowd funding campaign for something it at the time called “Nimble 2”. Jumping from $17k to $265k this time there was clearly something here people were finding interesting. Building on those modular rules the goal was to create a full TTRPG with the same core mechanics but with its own take on the popular archetypes veterans of games like D&D and Pathfinder would recognise as well as “streamline” much of the more cumbersome elements of games like 5E especially with its roughly 500 spells! “Rules tight, not rules light” was the tagline though before any alarm bells start ringing here. 

Nimble's three books - Photo by Vre'Kais

What we got was an adorable set of three books, a GMG, a Core Rules, and a Heroes book of classes. They are truly some of the prettiest TTRPG books I’ve ever bought and they are full to the brim with all the information you need to run a game of Nimble. Excellent GM advice, some adventures to try out, monsters, spells, even a surprisingly detailed little setting to start you off if so inclined.

So why am I talking about Nimble now? There’s a few reasons for that actually. Firstly a few months ago I wrapped up a 5E campaign that I converted to Nimble part way through, something that worked surprisingly well but I won’t pretend didn’t present its own challenges. Secondly Nimble just completed its third crowd funding campaign for “Monsters and More” ending with a staggering $1.1 million in backing. This new book intends to expand the current mini bestiary extensively as well as adding a Nimble take on a long list of concepts such as companions, dungeon crawls, bastions, traps, and much more. In addition to this new book though there’s a reprint of the current books, as well as new Hero “zines” which condense all the information you need to run a class into its own booklet, letting a GM throw them on a table and have players pick one and get playing. I highly recommend checking that out, as of writing late pledges are still being accepted.

The Flow

The current rules I’ll admit are focused on tight tactical combat. There’s no haggling systems here, or complex social systems. The game relies on a condensed skill list and a few choice abilities for out of combat interactions. You can roleplay freely in Nimble but if you want a narratively driven system this is a very light touch game in that respect. As a GM I love this though, I can slot in any role play mechanisms I want or just rely on skill checks without getting in the way of how Nimble wants things to work.

Nimble is fast but maybe not in the way some might expect. A player’s turn will probably take just as much time in Nimble as it does in a game like 5E, with the possible exception of a spell caster who hasn’t read their spell list yet. The speed of Nimble is in how much progress actually occurs within the turns. I found Nimble when looking for a system that avoided “nothing happened” turns, and they are far far rarer in Nimble. A combat in 5E that might take 6 rounds could be as few as 3 rounds of Nimble because progress is almost always occurring.

This article won’t go into exhaustive detail about every mechanic but here’s some highlights of the system that I enjoy. Be warned that I will probably make a number of comparisons to D&D 5E which for my sins is the only other TTRPG I’ve spent a significant time playing.

The Attack Roll

This is probably one of the biggest changes out of the lot. In a 5E game you roll to hit, you tell the GM what you rolled, they consult a stat block, tell you if you hit or miss, you roll some damage, tell that to the GM, they mark it off. Tried and tested formula but with a lot of back and forth. In Nimble Heroes just roll damage. If they roll a 1 it misses, if they roll the max value of their weapon’s die it’s a CRIT and they roll the die again (and keep doing so until they don’t roll max). Then they tell the GM the damage they’ve just done. No back and forth, it’s just done. Next attack. 

What I love about this is what it means for weapons with different die sizes. A dagger with d4 damage has a 25% chance of missing, but also a 25% chance of getting a CRIT. Want something more reliable, not a problem grab a d10 Battleaxe for a 10% chance of missing. In my experience these miss probabilities are already far lower than most characters I’ve played anyway.

But what do attributes do if not determine hits? They’re still present; the game has four STR, DEX, INT, and WIL. They still modify weapon attacks too but they just add damage to them, so a Shortsword is 1d6 + DEX and a Spear is d10 + STR. How exactly these work I’ll cover in a future article when I talk about monsters though, they have tricks of their own.

Actions

In Nimble every player has 3 actions per turn. No special types or anything but some things do use more than one action like some of the larger spells or big attacks like the Commander Class’s Sweeping Strike ability to just hit everything in one bit sword swing. You can even use the same action more than once, you could move 3 times if you wanted (it is an action in Nimble), or even attack 3 times. Attacking multiple times in the same turn though has subsequent attacks be considered “Rushed” which incurs stacking disadvantage penalties. Yes, it stacks.

Advantage and Disadvantage in Nimble can escalate, for Advantage you add more dice to the roll and remove the lowest, and for Disadvantage you remove the highest. It won’t be every roll that you have 3+ lots of Advantage on, that’s not common at all, but it does allow for a few more interesting team work opportunities where multiple sources of advantage aren’t wasted. Great for when something really needs to die right now.

In my experience having this singular action type really speeds up the turn, players aren’t scouring the sheet looking for a specific type of action to use just because that’s all they have left. Then at the end of your turn you regain 3 actions 

The Reactions

Reactions are where the team work element of Nimble is most evident but also where it diverges from other games I’ve played the most. 

Defend

Armour in Nimble isn’t some passive thing that makes you harder to hit, an abstraction that in my opinion already stretches my imagination and often led to me describing “misses” as near hits when I was still playing 5E. Instead you have the Defend reaction, it uses 1 action and allows you to subtract your armour for an attack that just targeted you. I like this abstraction but I’d be lying if it wasn’t at least a little divisive amongst some players. It does mean that armour unless you defend isn’t offering any extra protection but I personally think that simulates armour better than other approaches I’ve seen. It’s a tool you use actively, you choose to put your armour in the way of an attack. It keeps players focused on the combat state, you can only defend once per round and it uses up an action (meaning you’ll start your next turn with actions already used). You need to see what’s happening and make a tactical decision on using it or not.

I personally love this because it’s far closer to my own head canon on what HP actually represents. It’s a sort of battle stamina, not a numerical representation of how healthy someone is but instead how long they can fight and defending against a big attack can keep someone in the fight longer but might mean some sneaky small attacks get you in the shins.

Interpose

This action should just be in more games as it really lets players fulfil that heroic defender archetype like no other, and it’s not something you have to wait to take a feat for. You can just jump in front of a nearby ally and become the target of an attack. Want to save the squishy mage with your massive HP pool? Go ahead. Want to make the heroic sacrifice so that the Shepherd can heal everyone next turn? Please. It’s one of the best reactions in the game and I love that everyone gets it.

Help and Opportunity Attacks

These are still here with their own Nimble twists. Help is Nimble is specifically a reaction that lets you give any other player advantage if you can “reasonably explain” how you might do that, not too dissimilar but I feel a bit more explicit in it being something players can do in combat.

Opportunity attacks however are largely the same, though they’re at disadvantage now. This small change, in combination with monsters not getting to make Opportunity Attacks makes movement a far larger part of the game. Less running to the middle and forming a line, running up and then looking for an advantageous place to end the turn is available to both heroes and enemies.

Wounds and the “Dying” Condition

Continuing the HP is stamina abstraction brings us to wounds and “dying”. Dying is a condition in Nimble that heroes are afflicted by whilst at 0HP. You’re not out of the fight unconscious, you still get a turn but you can only use one action. You can even still attack with that action but there’s a STR save to see if you over exert yourself and take a wound.

Wounds are like 5E Exhaustion but without the massive stacking consequences, there is a variant rule where they cause -1 to all skill checks but this was demoted to a variant for good reason in my opinion, namely that having any wounds at all is surprisingly close to being dead. For most heroes (some ancestries and backgrounds increase this) taking 6 wounds kills you, and in Nimble death is a bit more permanent than it might be in other games. There are some spells to bring back the dead but none of them are trivial to use, most of them can only resurrect someone once, some of them are secret spells not even shown to the players.

When you’re reduced to 0HP you gain the Dying condition, and take 1 wound. If you’re hit whilst dying you take 2 wounds, or 3 if you’re CRIT. As a GM it took me a while to adapt to viewing Heroes at 0 as still being in the fight, something I still do based on enemy intelligence levels. Being at 3 wounds is half way to dead, and one CRIT could end it all. The net result of these mechanics is that healing before Heroes are reduced to 0 has far larger incentives than in other games. Avoiding dying avoids losing a lot of actions, and gaining a wound that can only be removed by a Safe Rest. 

That wraps up my favourite bits of Nimble’s Combat Flow, there’s still lots of detail I’ve skipped over here so if you want to know more I highly recommend the books. Come back next week for my thoughts on Nimble GMing!
