Managing Burnout in Umamusume: Pretty Derby and Kentucky Derby Week!

April 27th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Perhaps you saw the social media posts, Reddit threads, or similar, but the long prophesied Trackblazer Burnout has seemingly officially hit the Global Umamusume servers, leading to numerous players dropping the game or announcing long retirements until the next scenario drops in July, if they return at all. While this was something a lot of longtime fans had feared, it seemed like at first Global might escape the fate of Japan’s release of the game, where the player count nosedived for the nearly six months that Trackblazer (then Make a New Track) ran wild on the game. Certainly there are a lot of valid complaints to be made about Trackblazer, one of them being that the competitive side of the game, Champions Meetings, have become almost entirely dominated by whale players and a sadly far more stale meta than one might have hoped.

While Global has the benefit of faster overall schedule and thus less time with Trackblazer at the center of the game, we’re still about 3 months away from the next scenario making an appearance, and in the meantime, that could result in a lot of loss of interest, progress, or even rewards for the players. So, this week, we’ll be taking a look at ways to help navigate burnout with the game and keep interactions with it healthy so that when new content does arrive, you’ll be primed and ready to jump on board with it without having to have lost your momentum, club membership, or other benefits! And, we’ll also be taking a moment to give a little shout out to the big real life event of this week: The 2026 Kentucky Derby!

Economic Management



The biggest problem with dropping out of the game completely is the loss of free income. Although the dailies can be tiring, especially considering how long Trackblazer takes to complete, it isn’t the end of the world if your daily routine consists of logging in, doing your Team Trials and Daily Races, and checking in with your Club. Although you won’t be reaping the full daily benefits of a full schedule, staying connected to the game even in a minor way is going to pay out a lot more in the end than the few extra carats you’ll get from forcing yourself through Trackblazer every single day. Also, you can complete your daily in a few creative ways if you really want to: one of them involves simply failing a career as fast as possible (which isn’t really ideal), or just… playing another career scenario!

If you’ve been focusing on training your Umas for PVP, there is a good chance that you’ve received a lot of new trainees that you’ve not taken through their storylines at least once, and the current downswing in the game’s interest is a great time to actually accomplish this by playing through the URA Finale Scenario at least once with each trainee. You’ll get to see their actual story, which you can play at your own pace, and the scenario length is fairly simple. URA is also a pretty easy option for parent Sparks if you’re looking to build up a basic array of parents. If you’ve never lucked into 6 or 9 Spark parents in a certain stat, URA can help you get those easier by being easier to run and quicker than other scenarios. Once you have those parents, you can always move on to making more advanced ones in Unity or Trackblazer respectively, but URA is still an easy way to engage the game and keep building your roster up.

There is also the fact that logging in daily helps maintain your economy through giveaways. For example, this week is Golshi Week, and the Golshi Week benefits for just logging in can add almost 5k carats to your account, which is 1/6th of a guaranteed SSR or trainee that you may want (being 30k carats). Something that will never change in Umamusume: Pretty Derby is that the rate for pulling who or what you want will remain the same, and that the best way to guarantee a 3 star Trainee or SSR card is to get it via the pity mechanic after 200 pulls, or 30,000 carats.

Measuring Fun Particles

One of the most tiring aspects of discussing live service games is the concept of fun and time investment, especially since fun is such a personal concept; frankly, if you are feeling that Umamsusume: Pretty Derby is no longer fun for you, nothing I am going to say or can say will convince you otherwise. I can at least promise that the next upcoming scenarios and a lot of the new things added around 1.5 and 2 year anniversaries for Global will be really cool, but even in that sense, taking a break will probably do more for you than not. There really isn’t anything wrong with taking time away from the game, especially since the game should be, well, fun, and if you are doing it as “work”, then there’s no way you’re going to enjoy it.

If you haven’t joined a Club, I highly suggest that you do so, and if you’ve noticed that your own Club is becoming a ghost town due to players dropping out of the game, you may want to start hunting for a new and more active one. Having other players to talk to and exchange notes with or talk about the game with is going to do a lot of work to help you out with staying interested, but it also helps prevent that sense of isolation that can set in when all you do is log in, get tired of the game, and log out. Having players to practice with, do Room Matches with, or other things can go a long way to making your connection to the game feel more fun. For example, having a group that all trains the same trainee for a room match without any real consequence is a fun way to give yourself a goal that isn’t as set in stone as PVP training or Spark farming.

As a final note, if you are a fairly new player or someone who picked up the game after it initially launched, the PVP Champions Meetings are going to be restarting in May, meaning that if you missed races or wanted to try your hand at doing better in them this time, you’ve got a second chance at trying out events like Taurus Cup; just keep in mind that these events will be rerunning under the current meta conditions, not the ones they initially launched with, meaning they can be even more competitive this time than they were the first time around!

Derby Week in the Umamusume Era

A real tweet in 2026.

If you’re looking for something a little fun this week to go along with your usual dailies and Golshi Week, then you’re in luck: this week is Derby Week, which means the Kentucky Derby is on the horizon. Perhaps you’ve been curious about horse racing since the game arrived last year, but sadly had done so a few weeks after the Derby and Preakness had run for the year, meaning that there was no large scale American race of note on the calendar. But this year, we’re primed and ready to celebrate the running of the Derby alongside Umamusume: Pretty Derby’s rising popularity, which means that it’s time to finally embrace the sport fully!

But how do you watch the Kentucky Derby? Well, you’ve got a few options in the United States: You can watch it on NBC, or through Peacock’s streaming service, assuming you aren’t going to be in Kentucky to watch the race live! The 152nd Derby is a lot more than the namesake race, though, so perhaps if you’ve never watched horse racing before, you should consider blocking off a good deal of your day to see the full card. Of course there’s nothing wrong with just watching the big race and calling it a day, but if you’ve ever been curious to see what horse racing is all about, the Derby is a great full card with a varied set of races that can give you a great overall experience that is one of the most easily accessible ways to watch horse racing (that being broadcast tv). If you're outside of the US, you'll probably have to rely on streaming, which I suppose can be sort of like the non-Japanese experience of trying to watch JRA races...

Churchill Downs, the site of the Derby, which also has a turf track on the interior loop.

The actual derby itself is a 2012~ meter race (so, a Medium, for you keen players!) run on Dirt at Churchill Downs, making it a pretty fascinating race considering that almost all big Japanese races are run on Turf almost exclusively, such as the Arima Kinen. Dirt can be a fairly difficult surface for horse racing, and last year’s Derby was a great example of that with the rainy conditions having reduced the dirt to a muddy mess and a chaotic overall race. This year’s weather forecast has a fairly clear but cloudy forecast (at least at the time of this publication), so I suppose we’ll just have to wait and see what happens when Saturday arrives. Also, if you’re really curious to check out the horse racing excitement, May 1st also has races, including the Kentucky Oaks, which are smaller purse but still exciting races to consider.

This year's competitors.

And if you were curious, Cygames is indeed set to sponsor another race this year at the Derby, this time on Derby Day itself! Which means that your chance of seeing Umamusume during the actual Derby is pretty high, all things considered, and I’d be lying if I said that it wasn’t a reason I’ll be watching on Saturday. If you’re looking for other Umamusume connections, the recently added in Japan Umamusume Forever Young’s jockey, Ryusei Sakai, will be racing in the derby on the 10th Post competitor Wonder Dean, whose grandsire is Deep Impact, a famously absent race horse in Umamusume’s catalog. Also, just so you’re aware, Post 7’s horse, Danon Bourbon, is unrelated to Mihono Bourbon, so don’t get too excited that everyone’s favorite Gundam Uma has a descendent in the mix.

As you may have noted, the Kentucky Derby is a long historied race, with this being the 152nd running of it; it is also the most watched and attended American race, which is a very important statistic to keep in mind when considering the race and it’s popularity. While it is a big deal in America, Japanese races actually often outspend and out attend the Derby, but that doesn’t mean it isn’t still a huge deal. If you’re looking to get in on the fun, there are a few traditions you could consider from the safety of your own home:

Sunday Silence winning the Kentucky Derby and securing his place in history. Fun fact: Manhattan Cafe is said to have looked so much like his father than he even played him in a movie, and the "friend" Manhattan talks to in her events is meant to represent the invisible Sunday Silence.

The Mint Julep is probably the most notable, an alcoholic drink consisting of bourbon, mint, sugar syrup, and served iced. The food option is a little less easy. Named Burgoo, the stew consists of chicken, beef, pork, vegetables, and is incredibly heavy and thick, so not exactly light fare for a day at the track (or your living room). Of course you could also opt to dress up with lavish fashion and, specifically, a giant hat, but that’s a mileage may vary sort of deal (I personally just opt for my various array of horse racing socks). Finally, you’ll hear the race sometimes referred to as the “Race for the Roses”, which is a reference to the prize the winner receives that consists of 554 roses woven into a blanket. Finally, you could consider the Kentucky Derby to be one of the most important starting points for this article existing in the first place: Were it not for the performance of Sunday Silence, who would go on to sire or grandsire almost the entire Umamusume roster (specifically giving a shout out to Stay Gold, who in turn gave us Goldship, Nakayama Festa, Dream Journey, Orfevre, and Fenomeno). Sunday Silence was certainly an odd horse, and his life is certainly a fascinating journey, one that culminated in his living in Japan and siring over 1500 foals (Agnes Tachyon, one of those foals, would go on to sire even more than that). The Derby and Umamusume are deeply linked, even though Cygames has yet to find a way to incorporate American races and horses into the game. For now, anyway…!

Keeping an Eye on May

Although April certainly came and went, we are continuing on with the Trackblazer era into May!
