Heroes of the Black Library: Minka Lesk

April 25th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Across the grim darkness of Warhammer 40,000, the Mortal Realms of Age of Sigmar, and the battlefields of The Old World, a ceaseless conflict rages between steadfast champions of order, terrifying architects of ruin, and everyone caught in between. Heroes of the Black Library is a deep dive into the incredible characters that make these universes so rich, from the tabletop to the bookshelf and beyond. Join us as we explore their bloody, larger-than-life tales, proving that in an eternity of carnage where there is no peace, the line between an inspiring hero and a monstrous villain is almost always forged in the fires of war. Happy reading!

Hello and welcome, folks, to the kickoff installment of our brand-new series: Heroes of the Black Library! Each will feature one of the heroes (or villains!) that have been brought to life in the pages of the Black Library, and today we'll be looking at the journey of Minka Lesk, Captain of the Cadian 101st Regiment's 7th Company.

Of course, she didn't exactly start out that way...

Image credit: Games Workshop

Cadia Stands (2017)

It can be difficult to portray a natural "rise up the ranks" in the Black Library, because to do so realistically requires the time and space to work in that only a novel series can best provide- and only a comparative few characters are afforded that much ink. William King's Ragnar Blackmane is a classic example, with the (recently re-released) six novel arc tracing his ascent from a simple human tribesman through the honors of the Astartes.

When we first meet Minka, it's as a humble Whiteshield. That's the term given to young Guard cadets, whether they be new conscriptions to reinforce an existing regiment or even the sons and daughters of stationed Astra Militarum troops.


Before Minka was born, her mother had birthed five children already, their fathers either killed or sent off-world. She had still had one more child to raise before she fulfilled the quota she had given herself. She had shown her zeal by producing two: Minka and her little brother, Tarli.

Her parents’ coupling had not been a result of a love-marriage, they had not believed in that. Just two young healthy and fertile warriors breeding the next generation. (from Traitor Rock)

Interestingly, Minka isn't the main character of Cadia Stands, as author Justin D. Hill took a more ensemble approach to telling the story of the fall of Cadia. We see the events unfold through a number of different sets of eyes, including a Kasrkin Captain, a General in the High Command, and an Armoured Regiment Major.

Then there's Minka. As a raw recruit, notes Hill in an interview, "all she ever wanted was to get off Cadia, and she can’t believe her bad luck to be born at this moment in time, knowing now that this will never happen."

But as they say, be careful what you wish for as you just might get it. As Heretic Astartes and their cultist rabble spill into her hometown of Kasr Myrak, the Whiteshield contributes to the Cadian resistance until the final evacuation order.

As they say, the planet broke before the Guard did, and Hill's influence on the subsequent Cadian narrative is not to be overlooked. As Lenoon noted in his omnibus review here on Tabletop Battles:


Hill has the honour of writing a book with a surprisingly large amount of influence on subsequent developments of Imperial Guard lore - how much of this was at the behest of editors and how much was Justin Hill I don’t know - but the long tail of "Cadians wondering what the hell happens next" starts here, and that’s an achievement and a half.

The Minka sections, of a terrified Whiteshield fighting for her life in a hopeless retreat, are really good. There’s fire, faith, fury - and fun - here, lovely/horrifying description, good old fashioned bolter porn and character work.

In the novel's Epilogue, Lesk and the other surviving Whiteshields become full Shock Troopers.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Cadian Honour (2018)

Between the first two books Hill managed to flesh out Minka a bit more through a pair of short stories, with The Place of Pain and Healing marking a clear transition from Minka-as-ensemble-component to Minka-as-Main Character. That story sees her on the evacuation ship, the Space Wolves cruiser Fang of Fenris, grappling with the fact that her planet just died as they race for the (relative) safety of a warp jump out.

The second story, The Battle for Markgraaf Hive, is notable not for what is gained, but rather what is lost as Minka's unit, the Cadian 101st, is deployed to a hive world to help put down a heretic uprising. It's a bloody affair, with the Cadians taking heavy losses throughout the story, and it sets up the "battlefield promotion" our heroic Whiteshield is on the verge of earning.

If Cadia Stands was about a fresh-faced recruit, Cadian Honour shows Minka Lesk working through what it means to be a leader. The heavy losses they've been sustaining mean that there's a lot of green troops replenishing their losses, and the now-Sergent Lesk must mold them into an effective fighting force as they take on a heretical rebellion that has broken out across the planet Potence.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Traitor Rock (2021)

The third book in the series employs the same device that Dan Abnett used to such good effect with his Gaunt's Ghosts books, completely changing up the field of battle to see how his characters will perform. Instead of a good ol' fashioned heretic uprising, the Cadian 101st must assault a traitor fortress that's proven all but impregnable over the course of a four-year siege.

From beach landings to bunker-by-bunker clearances to infiltration behind enemy lines, Minka and her troops run the gamut of operational stratagems. Set around five years after the Fall of Cadia, a more wizened and battle-hardened Lesk gets a field promotion to Acting-Lieutenant in the course of the siege, and full Lieutenant at the end of the book.


It had been a brief but terrible campaign. But the Cadians had conquered. They had crushed the rebellion. This meant that, at last, they could move on.

But none of that mattered to Minka now. Nor did the lieutenant stripes that she wore on her arm, and the confidence she had found in handling her troops in combat.

A trumpet blew as Father Keremm stepped forward and made a short prayer.

Once, Cadians had buried their own under Cadian soil in the fields of tombs she had trained beside as a Whiteshield. That was no longer a choice available to them. Their dead comrades lay wreathed in black shawls embroidered with the aquila and the Cadian Gate.

When Keremm’s prayers had come to an end the troopers of Seventh Company filed past, one by one, and threw in a handful of soil. (from Traitor Rock)



Image credit: Games Workshop

Shadow of the Eighth (2023)

By this point we've been with Minka Lesk for several years, both in-universe and without. Her rise up the ranks hasn't gone unnoticed, least of all by her in this relatable moment of imposter syndrome:


‘The stripes. How are they feeling?’ Sparker asked her as she finished her breakfast and slid her mess tray into the kitchen window.

‘Good,’ Minka said. She had stitched her captain stripes on the night before and, of course, they did not feel right. The thickly woven cloth was stiff. She felt its resistance every time she moved her arm.

He slid his own tray into place beside her and then felt in his breast pocket for a lho-stick. He offered her one and she shook her head.

‘Leading a company. I don’t know how I got here. I keep thinking some­one is going to catch me out,’ she said. (from Shadow of the Eighth)

Part of Hill's strength with these stories is his ability to weave Minka's own personal struggle "what does it mean to be a leader" with the broader identity of "what does it mean to be a Cadian," looking at how both evolve and change over time.

The anxieties and worries of a Whiteshield are much different from the burdens of command. The tension inherent in integrating non-Cadians into Cadian units has been the grist for other stories in the Black Library (see, for instance, The Strength of Symbols by Carrie Harris for the non-Cadian perspective), and now as a leader it falls on Minka to navigate integration for those under her command.


‘I feel like I’ve been in a museum,’ Minka replied.

‘Why?’

Minka paused. ‘He’s the past. All of it. Cadia. The kasrs. How long till the last of us is dead?

It’s all going to fade away.’ She sighed. ‘To be Cadian is something else now.’

‘And what is that?’

Minka thought for a long time. ‘I don’t know. It’s up to us to define it." (from Shadow of the Eighth)

In this way, Shadow of the Eighth is a transition book for Minka, and by the end of the tale- involving surviving a trap lain by the forces of Chaos- she gets her next promotion to Captain.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Hell's Last (2025)

And here it is, the culmination of Minka Lesk's trajectory as she manifests at the peak of her powers (spoilers ahead). One of the headlining books unveiled as part of the Black Library Celebration in 2025, Hell's Last sees the death of Lord General Isaia Bendikt. Bendikt has been a constant fixture in Hill's series, beginning as a Major and one of the main point-of-view characters in the first novel.

As she's risen up the ranks, so has he. When he and his Kasrkin are ambushed on a sweltering jungle world by the forces of Chaos, Minka leads a rescue squad to extract him. She arrives to find him mortally wounded, his last act to pass along his relic power sword, Paragon, to Minka.


Bendikt looked up at her, and Minka’s fingers closed about the hilt.

‘Cadia stands,’ she said, her eyes not leaving his, and he smiled before life finally left him.

Minka shook her head in dumb grief. Lord General Bendikt’s last battle had been fought. He had taken the Emperor’s Blessing. He’d fought the enemies of the Imperium all his life. The wars would never be over, but Bendikt’s part in them was done. (from Hell's Last)

The blade- "a broadsword that had been punishing the enemies of the Imperium of Man since the days of the Great Crusade" and originally the personal weapon of an Imperial Saint- is far more than just a weapon for Minka. It becomes a symbol, once which finally puts to rest the feelings of inadequacy and uncertainty that have dogged her steps her entire life. It also settles for her the question of Cadian identity, as she realizes that her soldiers have, like the blade, may come from different places but have been forged together into a weapon of war.

Naturally, the remainder of Hell's Last sees Minka avenging the Lord General's death, putting the forces of Chaos (literally) to the sword.

The Author

Reflecting on the career of Minka Lesk in the introduction for the Special Edition of Hell's Last, Hill touched on all of these elements for his most enduring main character.


As a Whiteshield, she was the lowest-ranking trooper, taking orders and proving herself. But in the mess that is the post-fall Shock Troop, she's been picked for rapid promotion, and risen quickly- perhaps too quickly for her comfort, which is great. Minka has to fit in, and must prove herself to the troopers who follow her as much as the people giving her orders. Leading a company is a lot of responsibility, and I like throwing challenges at all my characters. We learn a lot about them from the way they respond.

Minka's also starting to lose the fear that she is not worthy, though I think she'll always have the sense of decline that pervades the Warhammer 40,000 universe. That the new Shock Troop she has helped to build will never be the same as the old one. Maybe she's starting to think that's a good thing. She's still filled with faith, both in herself and in her regiment. She's not jaded.

Not yet.

Whether that's foreshadowing for a sixth Minka Lesk book is entirely a matter of speculation, though it does suggest a direction such a story might take. Regardless, Minka Lesk has become one of the most identifiable and recognizable Guard characters of the modern Black Library, and cemented Hill as one of the leading writers for the Astra Militarum in the modern era.

Hill's Cadian saga comprises about half of the novels he's written for the Black Library, and represents his most consistent body of work. His debut was 2016's Storm of Damocles, part of the Space Marine Battles series that focused on the Deathwatch going up against the t'au. He's also dabbled with Necromunda (2019's Terminal Overkill), Warhammer Horror (the excellent The Bookkeeper's Skull in 2022), and the Sisters of Battle (2023's Pilgrims of Fire).

If not a "happy accident," the character of Minka Lesk was a bit of serendipity that highlights how good things can emerge from having to start a book all over again- a bit like Dan Abnett's Lijah Cuu.

"I was starting work on a series of novels about Creed a couple of years ago," explained Hill in his Track of Words interview, "but when Black Library editorial went through a couple of somersaults and my editor switched positions in Games Workshop, then the project went from a series of novels to a long short story."


When the dust settled I picked Creed up again in 2015, and was starting work on a common Cadian’s retelling of Tyrok Fields when news came to me that Cadia was in fact going to be destroyed. This meant some drastic changes to the novel’s outline, and rather than try and patch it together, I went back to the beginning and rewrote the book entirely. All the work I’d done imagining Cadia away from Tyrok Fields – the life, the kasr, the culture – served as a great backdrop for this novel. But the only character who survived was Arminka, the young female Whiteshield. I kept her. She’s cool.

Games Workshop certainly agreed. So much so, that she's been cast into plastic form not once, but twice!

Image credit: Games Workshop

From Paper to Plastic

Minka's first miniature (above) was released as a Commemorative Edition hero alongside Shadow of the Eighth in 2023. This was a big splash from Games Workshop, accompanying a Special Edition of the book, the release of The Last Whiteshield omnibus, and even a downloadable battleplan for those wanting to recreate the Fall of Kasr Myrak on the tabletop1.

Then as part of the Black Library Celebration in 2025, they went even bigger.

Image credit: Games Workshop

By the time Hell's Last releases, Minka's already risen up the ranks so it's only fitting that she has her command retinue with her. From left to right, you see:
  • Grawnya Ironbrow. Originally of the Drookian Fen Guard (and subject of Hill's short story Fen Guard, released as part of the Black Library Celebration that same year), Grawnya first appeared in Shadow of the Eighth as Minka had to work to integrate non-Cadian remnants and replacements into the 101st.
  • Confessor Talbeas. Another Shadow debutant, she was part of the Ecclesiarchy attachment to the regiment but took on a more central role in Hell's Last. After picking up the banner as it fell in battle, she continues to carry the colors of the 101st.
  • Cpt. Minka Lesk. We see Minka here in her Hell's Last iteration, now a Captain in the Astra Militarum. Note too that she now carries the relic power sword Paragon, given to her by the fallen Lord General Bendikt.
  • Chief Commissar Shand. Unlike Grawnya and Talbeas, who are later characters in the series, Shand has been around since Cadian Honour and grown in importance as the series has continued. While something of a mentor figure for Minka, he's still a Commissar and brings the Summary Execution ability to the tabletop.
  • Veteran Trooper Jaromir. Like Shand, Jaromir's a longtime mainstay in the series. A trueborn Cadian, Jaromir has followed Minka as she's climbed up the command structure and presents a voice of grizzled experience.
The experience of having his characters immortalized in plastic has been a very proud one for Hill. "I was a gamer long before I was a Black Library author," he once noted, "and seeing Minka appear in plastic was an honour. I love people tagging me, showing me their own attempts at painting Minka, or photos of her leading armies on the battlefield. My son has Minka in his Cadian army, which is pretty extraordinary."

For those of us who enjoy collecting and displaying their Black Library books, there are a number of ways you can do it beyond just the miniatures (though of course, I'd always recommend those too!).

Image credit: Jay Kirkman

Shelf Candy

The deluxe edition of Hell's Last, shown above, follows the same style that was used for the Special Edition of Shadows of the Eighth. Both of these are now only available on the secondary market, of course, but those new to collecting may want to check out our Beginner's Guide to Collecting the Black Library here.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Next, an easy grab to build your Cadian-themed display is this in-store collectible coin, "Cadia Stands," released in January of 2023. Most of these coins tend to trade at around the USD $15-20 mark in the aftermarket.

Image credit: Games Workshop

February 2025's coin was that of the Kasrkin mask, another easy add.

Image credit: Games Workshop

Official licensee Starforged makes a lot of terrific Warhammer curios that pair very well with books in a library, including this metal pin badge.

Image credit: Starforged

They also make these commemorative rings which, while pricey, are absolute eye-grabbers in any decorated library.

Image credit: Starforged

Finally, another official licensee Warhammer Art produced a limited series of art prints for Traitor Rock, and they're amazing. One can easily imagine just this kind of Imperial propaganda being produced in the grim, dark future (and for a good look at the Imperium's propaganda machine, check out Outgunned or Above and Beyond by Denny Flowers).

These were limited to a print run of 1250, and are only available on the aftermarket now. I couldn't source an original image, but they do pop up on eBay from time to time.

Image credit: Splitzbox

Cadians are in many ways the "default" Astra Militarum now, so you should have little difficulty finding things to add to your bookshelves to help bring them to life. But for me, it always starts with the paper, then the plastic... then the metal.

I hope you've enjoyed our new Heroes of the Black Library feature, and we'll be back soon with a turn towards the Mortal Realms!
