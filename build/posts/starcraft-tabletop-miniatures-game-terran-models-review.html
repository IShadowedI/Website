StarCraft Tabletop Miniatures Game Terran Models Review

April 28th, 2026 via Goonhammer
The SCVs are good to go and we’re in rear with the gear -- "the gear" being a model review for the current Terran range from the StarCraft Miniatures game. At the time of writing this, we have Jim Raynor, Marines, Marauders, Medics, and the Goliath to go through (with the latter being a standalone release outside the founder’s edition box). Let's take a look.

General Thoughts

Goliath Online Credit: Mildnorman

Norman: All the Terran kits come on colored plastic sprues, generally split by the blue “main” sprues and the clear plastic “effects” sprues. The clear plastic is pretty drastically orange (except for medics, where its green), which is great for the visors present on Terran marines and vehicles, but it works a little less well on the minerals that come in the box for basing or the weapon effects. I’m of two minds about the clear plastic bits. On one hand, they’re a neat inclusion and really help make the models look great unpainted by just adding some visual interest. That said, I think a lot of folks are gonna prime over most of them and paint them up so it looks consistent. My brain can’t personally square the circle of natural light reflecting off the glossy surface of a plastic visor vs the painted on lighting I’m accomplishing with the highlights. I think for some folks it’ll work great but it's just not for me.

Model quality wise, most of these go together really nicely. The joins all make sense and are pretty straight forward to do with a good amount of options in each kit. In terms of cleaning and mold lines, I’d compare it to Warhammer models from 5-10 years ago. There are some pretty visible and prominent mold lines that you’re gonna have to clean before you prime your models. That said, the sprue layout for the most part works really well, and the sprue has arrows next to the numbers so you know what pieces go to what designations.

It’s also worth mentioning that every kit in the range gives you models for a full reinforced unit, which means if you buy two boxes of Marines you can get three minimum sized units. This is really consumer friendly since it means you won't have to buy a whole box for three more dudes.

Craig: The metallic blue sprue does make finding sprue scars a little difficult, as the color isn’t 100% consistent. It is also a bit of a pain to see mold lines, but thankfully there doesn’t seem to be too many on these sprues. I mostly find them on the hoses of models like the Marauders.

I didn’t find any reason to do subassemblies with any of these models, aside from the riot shields. Everything seems like it will be easy to reach with a brush.

Contemptor Kevin: Honestly I think the metallic blue plastic/orange translucent plastic is pretty great. As noted, these are big chonky fellows cast in shiny metallic blue plastic and even assembled unpainted feel like something I would be comfortable playing with. The shiny metallic blue plastic was a lifesaver because when I would drop parts, I could easily find them on the hotel room carpet. The grey plastic from GW just kind of blends in and losing small bits from, say, a Saturnine Dreadnought is terrifying. Not so Terran plastics.  Like Craig I didn’t wind up doing any subassemblies on these guys, and my airbrush was able to hit everything I wanted to hit.  

One quick thing to note, since we got asked about this on the Discord: All of the bases have a slot that can fit a 5mm x 1 mm magnet. These work great for the 32mm and 50mm models. For the 80mm model I wound up using a GW base and put a pair of 5mm by 3mm magnets and these were secure in my Tablewar Magnetic case. 

Marines

Credit: Contemptor Kevin Stillman

Norman: The marine kit is pretty straight forward. There’s six poses here, three on each sprue sheet, and in the standalone box you get a repeat of the first sprue with the grenade launcher. There are a few in here with alternate poses and weapon options so you can keep things fresh as you go. Everyone has all the wargear they’d be able to upgrade too (shields and grenades for everyone, rocket launcher for one dude) but since WYSIWYG isn’t really enforced in Starcraft, you can kinda just put on whatever looks cool.

I have some minor nitpicks with this kit. First, the shield kinda just glues onto the shoulder, in a way that's kinda prone to popping off and looks a bit goofy. That said, that's how it works in the game, so I get it. A lot of the extra wargear has this problem where it just kind of glues into place with minimal guides. I didn’t have a ton of problems with things sliding around though, including on the bayonets for the gun which I figured would be a major issue.

My biggest issue with the kit is one inherent with the design of the marine power armor as a whole. Because the head and visor are attached to the chest. It’s really hard to tell where a model is facing with the visor on. This means an aimed gun to the side with a weapon effect glued on can easily look like some guy accidentally shooting to the side without looking. If you keep the visor off, the head of the marine is so embedded in the armor, it’ll be hard to get a brush in there, and there’s no way to sub assemble out the head and add it in later.

All that said, this kit is really solid. There's enough here to give each marine some character without them being festooned with doodads and bits that’d get in the way of painting 'em up fast. In addition, once I had built the first marine, I could build every other one in the set without instructions. They all follow the same patterns and all the pieces are clearly labeled as marine A, B, C, and so on, so they slapped together real quick

Contemptor Kevin: I left the shields off and in a trashcan at the City Center Hilton in Milwaukee. They didn’t really connect well with the rest of the model, and in my opinion would not photograph as well as I would like.  

Once I got the hang of building the kit, each Marine took about 20 minutes or so to build. They fit together really well and have some solid heft. Each figure has 10 or 11 key parts (the head, lower torso x2, upper torso x2, arms x2, legs x2, gun, and visor). Of the six Marine sculpts, two have alternate poses so your initial squads do not look identical. I agree with Norman that the "additional" wargear bits could have used better guides or contact points, but given the toughness of the kits and plastic they held on together during their trip through the MKE to DCA baggage claims.  

My biggest issue with the kit was cleaning them: The gates on the sprue are thick. My clippers were able to get them off, but then I had to spend some time and effort cleaning the nubs off of each and every part. As I became more familiar with the models and the layout it sped up though: mold lines for these figures are in the same place for each model, so it becomes second nature.  
Craig: I’d like to call out the missile launchers as the only part of the build that gave me any trouble. It was kind of difficult to figure out the orientation, and they really don’t slot in all that convincingly - even on the instructions they look a little misaligned. There also isn’t really anything that defines it, but the optional pistols are called Slugthrowers and represent that upgrade. As Norman said, WYSIWYG doesn’t exist as an explicit rule, so feel free to glue them on.

Terran Marine by Craig "MasterSlowPoke" Sniffen

Jim Raynor (with Point Defense Drone)



This is Jimmy - Jim Raynor by Craig "MasterSlowPoke" Sniffen

Craig: Jimmy here is pretty much an identical build to the standard Marine kit, so if you end up with a bunch of him, you could swap his head with a different marine to use as a sergeant or something. There’s two gun options - either a regular Gauss rifle held aloft or a sniper rifle held in both arms. There’s also two scenic bases on the sprue - a rocky outcrop or a space station platform. His head is detailed enough, and the hero rock base helps his identical armor from getting you confused on the battlefield.

The point defense drone is an extremely easy build - it’s mostly just a bunch of overlapping circles. They got economical with the sprue space, and it has a number of clear orange parts that are just supposed to be opaque, so be sure to not mask them when painting. This was the one exception I had to no subassemblies - it is dead easy to build so I just have nearly all the pieces separate for painting. I think it would be a nightmare to try to paint completely assembled.

Credit: Contemptor Kevin Stillman

Contemptor Kevin: I’ll echo Craig when it comes to the Point Defense Drone. The one thing I’ll warn with it is that you would want to mask the top of the orange smoke cloud because the fit connecting it with the Point Defense Drone’s parts is pretty tight.  

It’s worth noting that under the rules for this game, you don’t have to actually put Raynor on either of his sculpted bases. He connects to them via nubs in his boots and both boots stand flat on the ground. So if you were more interested in your own basing scheme and neither of the sculpted bases worked for that, you can avoid using either of them and just use a 50mm base of your choosing. 

Marauders



Kaboom, baby! - Starcraft Terran Marauder by Craig "MasterSlowPoke" Sniffen

Craig: These are big silly models. They stand about twice as tall as a regular Terran marine, and probably take up about 6-8 times the volume. Each of the two sprues build a single model. I don’t think they are labeled A/B, but it is possible I missed it. Regardless, it should be pretty easy to figure out which one is which, as the numbers are different between similar parts. 

Each model has three different sets of arm poses available. They’re not crazy different - there’s only so many different ways an egg shaped guy can hold two big gun fists, especially when the torsos are static. I am glad that they give the option, though - if it was just two sculpts for every single model when you could have like 12 of them it would get very samey, very quickly.

Sucks to be you! - Starcraft Terran Marauder by Craig "MasterSlowPoke" Sniffen

You will end up with 8 spare arm sets for every two Marauders. I can’t really think of anything they’d be useful for, maybe some engine bits for terrain, or just something to donate to your local Ork Mek for some scratchbuilds.

For the particularly Astartes-pilled among us, you will be a little mystified by the shoulder pads. They go the opposite way than you’re expecting. I do like that the glue point is where the pads’ models clip into the body of the Marauder, it is kind of a cute touch and provides a solid glue point. You’ll know when you’ve got them in the right orientation, they’ll click in real well.

I honestly thought they were a little too silly when I was building them, but after painting one I kind of fell in love. They just look really big and imposing in a cool way.

Credit: Contemptor Kevin Stillman

Contemptor Kevin: These are chonky, fun to build, and very goofy. I think the arm construction winds up being the weakest part of this build: because the arm joint wants to fit well for three different arm poses, the connection points for the arms is not quite as strong as most of the other fits in these kits. The left arm on Marauder B was the big challenge, as it wanted to slide off while the plastic glue was bonding the parts together.  

Norman: While these were fun enough to put together, I really don't love the poses on these guys. I guess gun hands are probably difficult to model elegantly but it feels like their arms are pretty far out for such a massive gun which makes the whole model feel off balance.

Medics

Starcraft Terran Medic by Craig "MasterSlowPoke" Sniffen

Craig: These are very similar to the Marine build, but a little simpler as the torso is 3 parts instead of 5. Despite being in the same kind of armor, they’re about 10% smaller than normal Marines. They are similarly straightforward a build as the Marines. The only tricky part is aligning the little health gun cannon to the arms - they’re all in a Geneva code complaint clear green color, so it’s a little hard to see the little mounting tabs, but not too bad.

There is no option to have them shoot anything but little plus signs. It’s a surprisingly wacky touch that I am sure will be a little bit of a controversial decision. It would be nice if there was a health goo/spray that didn’t have them, but you can also just cut off the firing effect if you really hate it.

The worst part of the kit is that the heads are completely incompatible with the normal Marine kit, so you can’t have a grizzled bald guy as a Medic, or a pouty lipped, gun-toting psychopath who can’t find the Any Key on a keyboard. Very disappointing.

Contemptor Kevin: The challenge I had with these guys was the fact the Medic Clear Sprue was green, and I did not realize that when I split my box with Craig at the end of Adepticon. So the Medics didn’t get built and painted alongside the rest of my Terrans over my birthday weekend. Whoops!

The shields for the Medics are different than those for the Marines: whereas the Marine shields feel like an afterthought that fit awkwardly (at best) for those models, the shields for the Medics have solid connection points and fit quite well. I'm not a huge fan of the tanks shooting plus signs, and so followed Craig's advice to cut them off.  

Goliath

Credit: Contemptor Kevin Stillman

Contemptor Kevin: While not the first model from the range I built, it was the first model that I finished. This is a big piece of plastic, about the size of a Redemptor Dreadnought (except crossed with an AT-ST Walker). Once assembled (it took a couple of hours), I knew it was going to be the first model I painted for this line and it paints up quick.

But the elephant in the room is the assembly. For the most part, StarCraft Terran models go together like a dream for all the important sections and bits. The Goliath is like that in almost all respects except for one part of the model: The hips. Unfortunately, the hips are the load-bearing part of the model.  

The way the hips are constructed is that the model’s crotch has a pair of parallel bars on each side. The leg connects to the bar via a C-Grip that the instructions warn you not to glue to the hip. This is because the leg has notches to pose your Goliath’s legs in specific ways. I think the idea is that the grip on the legs, when glued onto the base, would naturally settle into an optimal position. This design has two flaws. The first one is conceptual: If you don’t want to glue the model to the base for construction because your Terran paint scheme relies heavily on OSL from the base, it’s not going to have that stability. Secondly, and I don’t know if this was just my model or if Norman or Siegler dealt with this in their builds, but the C-Grips in the thighs did not connect well with the bar. It felt like the bar was a smidge too thick to have a secure grip. I eventually just glued everything to a base and then together and it worked out fine.

Craig: The way I kind of did the leg assembly was to glue the shins together, but leave the knee with the gears unglued. Then, with the feet roughly positioned where you want, put some plastic glue in the sockets for the feet and the hip joins on the top of the shins. You can then take advantage of the slow drying time to pose the legs how you want. Once it’s in the desired position, leave it alone for a while until the glue sets. Then, you can pull the legs off of the c grips, then put some glue in there and wait a while for this glue to harden. Finally, pull out the knee joins and glue those. I would also wait to do the underslug gun until the end.

This is the one model where I really wish they used the original StarCraft design. The SC2 Goliath is a very standard battlemech that you’d find anywhere, while the classic version is a wild Mad Maxian contraption that I just love dearly. This kit does come with the coolest firing effects - the missiles billowing smoke look great. The pilot inside also is well detailed, but you’ll probably never see him painted. You can leave the cockpit unglued I guess if you want to see him, or if you want to leave the orange canopy clear.
