Tabletop Battles Reviews: Inquisitor Kroyle & Intranzia Fraye Models

April 25th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Thank you to Games Workshop for providing us with these models for review.

Rejoice, for GW has brought us some more Imperial inquisitorial and ecclesiarchical weirdos in the form of Inquisitor Kroyle riding his alien beasty and Sister Dogmata Superior Intranzia Fraye riding her stompy walking throne. I love an Imperial weirdo, but how do these two stack up?

Inquisitor Kroyle

Cronch: When you need an Imperial weirdo, and they just absolutely have to be as strange and esoteric as possible, look no further than the ranks of the Ordo Xenos. Inquisitor Kroyle is the latest addition to their ranks, striding in on a…horse? Bug? Bug horse? Mount of suspicious origins, at the very least. There’s some really great storytelling going on in this sculpt, from the Inquisition brand on the beast, to the Imperial scope and strap jury-rigged to the xenos rifle, to the baffling array of gizmos and esoterica strapped around the luggage.

Kroyle goes together well, and can also be kept completely separate from his mount for ease of painting which is always appreciated. The reins are a little fragile, but do slot into place reasonably robustly when he is assembled into his final position. For painting, you may want to leave the mount off of the impressive hero rock, as it does make getting a brush between its multitude of legs quite difficult. I didn’t, and I regretted it a little. Unfortunately, doing this will also mean leaving a couple of feet attached to the rock, so do remember to paint those!

Inquisitor Kroyle. Credit: Rich Nutter

This model is a painter’s dream (or nightmare, depending on how you look at it). There are just so many different colours and textures. It’s one that will definitely reward careful planning and attention, and thematically at least it’s not unreasonable to expect an Inquisitor to carry a loud array of clashing. As I had opted to spend as much time as possible on Commissar Yarrick, I opted to take a speed-paint approach to Inquisitor Kroyle - I think it’s nice to show off a variety of techniques on the site, and that you can get a decent result without committing tens of hours.

I started with the beast, layering a few different blue-green contrast paints, before picking out everything non-metal across the model. I filled in the metals (his bright armour is Scale 75 Dwarven Gold), and did some layers and highlights on various areas, before finally adding a unifying shade of Army Painter Grimdark Shadow. He’s not going to win a Golden Demon, but after a matt varnish he’s more than ready to hit the tabletop! A really fun model and a great addition to your collection of Imperial Agents.

Intranzia Fraye - credit: keewa

Intranzia Fraye

keewa: What. a. model! What could be more Warhammer than a walking mechanical throne with a gothic arch roof, replete with rose window detail, bas-relief saintly imagery, skulls, twin heavy bolters and flamers, rockets, and a cherub? This model is so incredibly Ecclesiarchy that it’s almost painful. Sister Dogmata Superior Intranzia Fraye’s chair has a pretty clear callback to the old Inquisitor Karamazov miniature (it’s a walking chair, after all) and also, via the gothic arch roof and embellishments, to the Penitent Engines (some of Warhammer’s grimmest, darkest, most disturbing models). The Sororitas relaunch was the thing that really got me interested in 40k (before that I was pretty much a fantasy guy), and although I’ve had cause to lament the amount of extraneous detail on the rank-and-file models, here that extra care is absolutely warranted. 

As much as I love Morvenn Vahl and her giant golden Nundam (and I do), honestly something like this walking throne feels more appropriate for a high lord and general, and although I’m sure Intranzia can mix it up in a melee, her lofty perch and “you there, kill that heretic” pointed finger really project that air of authority.

The kit goes together very nicely, I didn’t notice any significant gaps, or reach any points where I thought “ugh this is a pain in the arse” I got in a bit of hot water in my last model review by making bombastic, strident, tongue–in-cheek statements for humorous effect, so let me speak on behalf all of the angry people of Reddit when I say we all actually love building Warhammer (it’s the smell of that Tamiya Extra Thin pickling my brain, you see). Obviously, you’re going to want to leave Sister Fraye off her throne for painting, otherwise you will find it completely impossible to paint the details behind her chair (that you can see but not reach) when she’s ensconced in place. 

Intranzia Fraye - Credit: keewa

I have a few rather minor gripes with this kit, however - the cowling for the heavy bolter/flamer array features the Order of Our Martyred Lady symbol, which is fine, but it would have been nice to have options for the other Orders Majoris too. I know, you can say “but keewa, she’s an epic hero named character, therefore she’s only in the OOML” and yeah, I get that, although it feels like a bit of a cop-out. Honestly, I think this thing would have been better as an unnamed Dogmata Superior, with a few more options (alternate order symbols, maybe a choice of sponson weaponry), but that’s nothing deal-breaking. My other gripe is that, while she has a lovely big pennant hanging down from her walker, with ample space for getting creative with transfers, plus a long scroll of parchment held by the cherub, the kit doesn’t come with a transfer sheet! It’s not like the box is too small for the transfer sheet - I wondered if I’d taken one out without noticing, but no, it’s just absent, which feels like a missed opportunity.

All-in-all, though, this is a great model and any Sisters player is going to want to grab one asap.    

TwoHorse: Intranzia Fraye is one of the coolest Sisters models yet released. She's everything I love about the range: metal as hell and totally ridiculous. I’m the sort of collector who generally aligns their collection with what works on the tabletop, and…that doesn’t matter here. She’s enough fun as a hobby piece that I won’t care if she just lives on the shelf.

Let’s start with the build. Unlike my esteemed colleague, I hate building models. Assembly is my least favorite part, because the perfectionist part of me that wants to paint well knows I should work in subassemblies, while the tryhard part of me that wants to play with my new toys is constantly rationalizing that I will absolutely be able to get a brush in there if I just glue it all together now and get started. This dynamic is compounded by the fact that the insane detail on even the most basic Sisters models is usually a tradeoff paid with a ludicrous number of fiddly parts cut on awkward angles (looking at you, Insidiants kit). 

Fortunately, Fraye is a breeze to put together. The mould lines are present but not oppressive (similar to a Mortifier for reference). Every piece fits together well, and most importantly there are no awkward cuts or weird pieces that force you to mask weird joins if you want to paint it without steady hands of steel. 

The components build into discrete sections, which makes it easier to keep all the textures separate. If you didn’t want to go this route, you could easily get away with building the whole chair/mech and just leave Fraye off the chair. Alternatively, put it all together and prime it black and leave the back of the seat in “shadow”. You do you. 

Paint-wise, this is a treat. The design provides an opportunity to experiment with texture on the throne itself, the braziers and candles are great for OSL tinkering, and the banner gives you a spot for freehand without being so large that it looks awkward if you just color it and move on. And of course, if you can’t get enough of the creepy babies and scowling ladies that define the rest of the faction’s coolest beats, Fraye’s got you covered.

Altogether, this is one of the best sculpts GW has released in a while. Unique, full of character, and works with you whether you want to spend real effort on it or just get it done. 10/10, would get pointed at again.
