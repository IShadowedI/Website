Start Competing: Five Reasons RTTs Are as Important as Any Other Event

April 27th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Reading the title of this article you probably fall into one of two camps:
  • The first camp are people who would call me crazy, and say that of course GTs matter more than RTTs - they are bigger, better, and are (typically) better-run events. 
  • The second camp are people who don't really understand what RTTs and GTs are, and why they'd be meaningfully different.
In this article I'm going to try and speak to both groups and dig into what RTTs are, why they matter more than you think they do, and why you should attend one as soon as possible, regardless of which group you fall into.

What Is an RTT?

If you are new to the hobby, then you may not understand what RTTs, GTs, and Majors are, or how they're different. So let's start with a quick primer:
  • RTT: Short for Rogue Trader Tournament. These are one-day events with three rounds. They typically have between eight and twenty participants, but can range higher: I have seen RTTs with as many as fifty people all showing up for one day of play! These tend to be run at local game stores and clubs.
  • GT: Short for Grand Tournament. These are two-day events with five to six rounds over two days. A GT typically needs a minimum of 28 players, but the ideal is 32 players for five rounds and 64 for six rounds - this is the number of players you need to have a sole undefeated player after five and six rounds, respectively. 
  • Majors and Super Majors: These events run three days and typically have 7+ rounds. A Major is an event with 100 or more players, while a Super-Major typically has 200+. Most majors and Super-Majors will either be eight or nine rounds, often with some kind of cutoff after the first few rounds to produce either a top 32 or top 16. Examples of Super-Majors are events like the Las Vegas Open, the London GT, or the World Championships of Warhammer.
While all of these are important, in this article we're going to focus on the smallest of these events, the humble RTT. While RTTs are often overlooked as the smallest events, I'm going to make the case for why you should care about them, with five key reasons why RTTs matter and you should attend them and not just focus on the big events.

1. An Introduction to the Game

While this is perhaps the most narrow of the benefits - RTTs have become increasingly important as an introduction to the growing Warhammer 40k competitive scene, and they're very accessible. GTs and Majors often cost hundreds of dollars to attend beyond their ticket cost, especially once you factor in costs for food, hotel, and travel. On top of that if you're traveling to one chances are good you'll have to take at least one day off work for travel to and from the event, and usually two or three if you're attending a three-day Super-Major.

For new players the prospect of investing that kind of time and money - you need a fully painted army for a GT - into an event you may not even enjoy, while taking time off work is a tough sell. RTTs solve all of these problems: for an RTT typically all you'll need is an army and $15-20 to cover the ticket price. RTTs often waive paint requirements as well, allowing for new players to really get a taste of competitive play or test out new units when they are still getting set up, which can be a major wall to climb when you are getting into 40k. Getting an army fully painted can take a year or more, especially if it's your first foray into miniature collecting and painting.

Having these smaller, lower stakes events is essential to bringing in new people in the competitive part of the hobby so that it can continue to grow. And it's worth noting how small the competitive scene actually is - for every competitive player there are a dozen more people who just collect models and paint, play Crusade, or play the occasional pick-up game with a friend. If you want the hobby to grow, you need approachable events for new players which can act as a safe introduction to competitive play.

YHP Alexandria AoS RTT

2. A Much-Needed Practice Tool

Unless you're a religious Table Top Simulator grinder like me, you need to find ways to test your skills and list ideas for bigger events - and RTTs are perfect for that. When it comes to practice, your options are essentially Tabletop Simulator, pick-up games at your local store, practice games with a friend, or RTTs.

Pick-up games, while potentially helpful, are by their nature less competitive than event games, which can have a significantly different feeling then a game where prize support is on the line. You also may struggle to find time to meet up with the competitive players in your area if they have conflicting schedules, reducing the quality of your practice. Likewise, practice games against a friend or teammate can be helpful, but scheduling and space are often major hurdles.

I'm a huge proponent of playing on Tabletop Simulator (TTS), but I will be the first to admit that playing a game in person can feel quite a bit different then picking up an army on a digital tabletop. I always advise playing with your army on the tabletop before attending an event, even if you have dozens of games with them under your belt with them on TTS. 

Which brings us to RTTs, my tool of choice when preparing for bigger events. When you attend an RTT you can expect three usually competitive games, preparing you for the kind of multi-round days you'll play at a GT. While there are often new players attending RTTs, you can usually expect that most players attending are experienced enough with their armies to give you good practice, and that will especially be true in rounds two and three. If you're lucky, you may also have one or two locals who are on par with or better than you; these are the games which really test you and help you improve - playing those players month after month at RTTs can really help you grow as a player.

Chase showing off his new trophy

3. Fuel for Competitive Drive

Odds are if you are into competitive Warhammer, you want to win, and that is totally fine. People should want to succeed in a competitive game. Here's the thing, though: You're not going to win GTs most of the time, even if you're good at the game. It's just math: GTs have a large number of players, and many of them are likely to be very good. It takes a combination of player skill, luck, and the right army to win a GT and it might be a long time before all three factors line up for you (Robnote: I didn't win my first GT until year three of the Road Through series).

RTTs are only three rounds and generally lighter in terms of competition (unless you're living in a particular hotbed - I attend RTTs with multiple Team USA players regularly), and that means they're much easier to mathematically win. Only having to win three rounds in a row to take home that first place position is awesome, and it's possible that your shark tank buddies may knock each other out in rounds 1 and 2, paving the way for you to fight for that top spot even if you wouldn't normally make the finals of a GT. Even if it's not a GT, winning an RTT can fulfill the competitive drive that you are looking for in Warhammer, rewarding you for growing as a player, who may one day make it to that GT finals table because of the time you spent honing your craft at a GT.

You also can take home prize support that you would not normally have access to. RTTs can have a wide range of prizing for players, so I cannot promise you anything, but usually taking down an RTT will earn you a decent amount of store credit at your local store, or even a small trophy or box of minis depending on the location. Even if you do not win, taking a podium finish often can earn you a small prize, which to be frank, feels awesome. Earning rewards for doing well at your hobby feels rewarding in a way that has to be experienced to convey. Between all the RTTs I have attended I have taken home quite a bit in prize support, and every time it feels great.  

Robnote: Most RTTs also do Best Painted scoring and may have prize support for that. This is another great way to get motivated - because RTTs are lax on paint standards, you can often make a good showing for Best Painted at your local RTT by just showing up with a fully painted army!

Andrew Nguyen examines a 3D Printed Thunder Hammer that Primaris Kevin made for Huzzah's Hammer in the New Year event at the September 2024 Huzzah Hobby Day. Credit: Kevin Stillman

4. Building Community

Let's say that you are not a new player, and you're at the top of your game and do not want practice, and you win every GT that you attend. Why should you care about RTTs? The answer is community: By far the most important reason that RTTs matter is that they are essential for building up local Warhammer communities. 

GTs are great for getting connected to the wider Warhammer world. I have played countless lovely people at these large events, and met friends and made connections that I will always appreciate, but those GT players go home, what's left is your local scene. You have to foster your local scene if you want to enjoy your time in 40k and have people to play with. I've moved across the country multiple times since I started playing Warhammer and each time I was able to get involved in the local scene because of my attendance at the friendly local game store's RTT. Each time I was able to speedrun my way into the inner circles of players by playing them and their friends at the local events. Those circles became friends with whom I could play games, lend models to and borrow models from, and travel to events.

Warhammer is a social game, and RTTs are a big part of how you enter and build upon that social scene. Without RTTs you could still build up local groups through play nights, Discord chats, or a pick up game here or there, but that competitive drive that we use to build each other up would be missing, making local Warhammer much more isolated. The best players in each town would focus on the next big event and no longer passing on their competitive knowledge to the local player base. If we want to improve ourselves as players while building up a stronger community behind us, you should attend RTTs.

The Outpost Sheffield - Host of the Ironborn Uprising Event

5. Supporting Your FLGS

Rob: This is an add-on to the community note, but I can't stress enough that just as important as building your local community and player base is showing support for the stores that host these events. Running a game store is not a lucrative business and it's fraught with all manner of risk and challenges, even for stores that are well run. Warhammer is, by most accounts, a very bad game to support from a business standpoint: Each table eats up 100 square feet of space that could otherwise hold a full draft pod of Magic players, and an RTT will take up four to eight tables of space for an entire Saturday. Plus, all of the terrain and mats for those tables need to be stored somewhere.

Supporting Warhammer is a big challenge for stores and if you have a local store that's got a good space and is run by good people, it's worth putting in the effort to support it.

Final Thoughts

No matter your skill level, RTTs are an integral part of the game we love. If you are a top-level player who has skipped the local RTT for a few months, take the time to stop by and help foster the competitive growth locally. If you are just getting into the competitive scene, sign up for the next RTT so you can get involved. If you are in a place with an RTT drought, talk to your local store and get one started, they really are not that hard to run. RTTs have something for everyone and I hope you are able to see that for yourself. As someone who attends two to three RTTs every month I just recently came to understand how important they are, and I hope that I was able to share that excitement with them with you.
