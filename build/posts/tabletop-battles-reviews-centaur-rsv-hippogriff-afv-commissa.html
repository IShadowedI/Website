Tabletop Battles Reviews: Centaur RSV, Hippogriff AFV, Commissar Graves, Commissar Yarrick Models

April 25th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Thank you to Games Workshop for providing us with these models for review.

Behold, a new bounty has been bestowed upon us. A legendary hero makes his return, a new hero carves out a space, and two new Astra Militarum vehicles roll off the production line and onto the battlefield, but how do they stack up?

Commissar Yarrick

Cronch: Even the mightiest Imperial heroes are liable to be laid low by a slugga round, choppa, or even a misdirected planetary bombardment, but as it turns out, death is no major imposition for an icon so singular as Commissar Sebastian Yarrick. The Hero of Hades Hive is back, striding onto the battlefield looking older and yet more imposing than ever.

Thankfully, with an icon as legendary as Yarrick the talented designers at GW reigned in any urge to put him in a mech, or turn him fully into a robot, and settled on a retelling of the previous sculpt that really does feel like the old lad has aged. His face is gaunt (no relation to other famous commissars) and he’s festooned with assistive technologies like a support for his power claw, and some mean-looking knee braces. He goes together easily, and parts are easily kept separate for subassembly painting if preferred - his head, storm bolter arm, base and backpack all have clearly-defined attachment points meaning he can be painted then assembled with little to no post-paint gap filling required. I did have to fill one seam down the side of his greatcoat, but nothing more egregious than you’d find on any model wearing a long robe. I decided to really pull out the stops on this model, taking inspiration from the Eavy Metal box art, and also a rare trip to non-metallic metal town.Unfortunately that does mean that I haven’t had enough time to finish him! Still, he’s coming along nicely, and I’m hoping to do this great sculpt justice when he’s finally done.

Commissar Yarrick WIP, showing subassemblies (and head attached). Credit: Rich Nutter

Andrew: Bale Eye, Old Man, Big Rick. The Hero of Armageddon has as many titles as you’d expect for someone who’s been in the 40k lore for over 20 years. For someone with such a storied history his design has changed very little from his previous iteration. His loadout is the same, the posing is almost the same barring a slightly different storm bolter pose. His power pack on his back is larger and connects to some exoskeleton parts that help support his iconic Power Klaw as well as braces on his legs somewhat hidden behind his coat. He of course comes posed on the usual Hero Rock to show he’s no normal bum.

Credit: SmearierHades

 His build is very quick and straight forward, a thing to be a little cautious of are the thin power cables that connect to his bionic eye and power supply, take care when cleaning mold lines. His power sword scabbard is also a little annoying to put on as the instructions have you sliding it under his tassels and it’s a little tight. His connection to his hero rock is actually pretty handy here. It’s got a long bit to lock into a hole in the base so even though I built and painted him without subassemblies, it was easy to pull him off the base when I needed to reach the inside of his coat and legs, and then have him secure on the base when I was painting the rest.  For the paint scheme I tried to stick as close to the box art as I could (and within my current paint skill level), although I’ve become a fan of hazard stripe power cables and chose that for his Bale Eye cabling.  As of now he is still not glued to his base as when it’s completed I want him to be able to be pulled off and placed on his Fortress of Arrogance.  

Commissar Graves

Bair: As Keewa goes into detail below about how the main chassis of Graves’ vehicle, the Centaur, is based heavily on real-world half track vehicles, what I want to point out is what makes it extremely 40k instead. That is, of course, incredibly over the top ornamentation in the form of a massive Aquila prow, huge minigun, and another very prominent Aquila on the front of the upper deck where Graves spends her time waving around a sword; one can only assume that she barks at the drivers to driver her closer to the enemy at all times. 

Credit: Bair

While I only had this kit to work on myself, it was very clear from the outset that it’s the same base kit as the Centaur (why wouldn’t it be, that’d be stupid) with a different second frame to make Graves and her specific variety of quasi-transport. For anyone that might have been hoping for Graves-on-foot to be a separate release later I’ve got bad news for you: that model is fully integrated on these large frames, so that’s not going to happen. From photos I thought the deck of this thing was going to feel really cramped but was pleasantly surprised how roomy it is for all of the crew! I pinned the crew members for ease of painting them separately even though sub assemblies are, naturally, the coward’s choice. 

Putting it together was fairly straightforward and actually one of the better experiences I’ve had with modern Warhammer kits (i.e. it wasn’t an insanely large number of pieces) and went together in about an hour. The one issue I found is that the front prow does not sit flush with the front of the tank. It just doesn’t. Knowing this going into it you might be able to shave down parts to make it sit flush instead, but it’s a little frustrating that an otherwise well designed kit has this really weird flaw to it. My only other gripe was with building the banner bearer, a large heavy banner like that should not have three separate connection points to the torso of the model, entirely separate from both arm pieces holding it. Insane. 

Credit: Bair

Graves on foot was very easy to put together, the Cadian helmet skull piece is completely separate and the stylised base is also entirely optional in the build; really nice if your basing doesn’t involve any ruins normally so that you can just skip it. 

My last complaint is in the rules section of this, as someone that doesn’t even play a lot of 40k it would have been much cooler if there was some kind of option for Graves to step down from her tank or to be placed on foot when the tank is killed! Both models come in the same box so there’s no excuse. 

keewa: I built the Gravesmobile and built and painted Graves on foot for this review. As noted by Bair, the base of the Gravesmobile is the Centaur RSV with significant alterations, in some cases which make it harder to actually assemble. Take the prow for instance, because you have to build the centaur’s large front radiator and then pop the cow-catcher prow on top, it doesn’t fit very well or sit particularly flush - the weird thing, though is that the entire top of the vehicle is different, including the bonnet, so there’s really no need for them to make you have to use the original radiator piece, it’s a little baffling.

As far as the crew goes, I agree with Bair on the absolute pain in the arse of her cheerleader flagbearer - I have to admit right away that I am not font of sculpted banners in general anyway, I find painting them to be a royal pain in the arse (skill issue, I know, I know) and would always rather have a flat, undetailed banner as an option (GW will never do this, I also know that). The banner bearer holds a big, very heavy flag (which sort of begs the question, when the vehicle is travelling at speed, how the hell is he able to stand on the top deck holding this huge flag and not get swept off altogether?) at an awkward sort of angle, in a way that’s not very stable, and if you cock it up with plastic glue you’re going to make a mess. I would have liked the option to skip the banner bearer altogether and just have him hold a rifle or something else, a vox caster maybe.

Commissar Graves - credit: keewa

Graves on foot is nice, although she’s nothing to write home about; in effect, she’s little more than an alternate commissar with a power fist, walking somewhat stiffly down a few ruined steps. It’s a fine character model, but there’s nothing very spectacular about it - imo she’s not nearly as cool as the other named woman commissar, Severina Raine (who seems to have been quietly put on a bus, since the prospective second novel never materialised, RIP). The star of the show is absolutely the Gravesmobile, her dismounted model seems like a bit of an afterthought to let you have a second foot commissar in a different pose. Since the attachment to the base is one foot, as Bair said, you could just put her on a regular base sans stairs and it would work just fine.

 

Centaur RSV - credit: keewa

Centaur RSV

keewa: When you think of the Armageddon Steel Legion, the first thing that springs to mind is “mechanised infantry”, and although not specifically Steel Legion, the Centaur is exactly what a mechanised infantry-focused army needs to have to rapidly redeploy all over the battlefield. “But keewa!” you might say, “Don’t the Astra Militarum already have a personnel carrier in the venerable Chimera?” and, well, yes they do - but in my opinion the Centaur is much cooler.

Centaur RSV - credit: keewa

As a half-tracked vehicle, the Centaur draws clear design inspiration from the M3 and Sd.Kfz. 251 of World War II, basically a large, powerful truck with tracks in place of the rear wheels. As with its historical counterparts, the troop compartment in the rear is open to the sky, while banks of viewing and firing ports are attached to the side on hinges (you have the option to have one of these ports open, if you choose), while the rear door/ramp clips into place without glue, so it can be opened and closed. 

Centaur RSV - credit: keewa

Although the datasheet lets you pack in 12 models, the model has four generic troopers to slot into the rear compartment (one radio operator, one shooter, and two others), while another can operate the pintel-mounted heavy stubber accessible from a hatch on top (I chose to leave this closed). There’s a non-optional piece representing a folded-up tarpaulin roof for the troop compartment - it feels like they missed a trick not including the option to have this roof extended and the compartment closed for people who don’t want to paint the troopers, but also don’t want to leave the compartment empty. 

In addition to all of that, the Centaur has the usual kinds of stowage you’d expect to find in a 40k AM vehicle kit, wrenches, axes, shovels, bags, jerry cans, fire extinguishers, etc., if you feel like it, your whizzy murder-van can be quite busy.

I love this truck, it goes together quite nicely, without any annoying gaps, with plenty of options to help distinguish them from one another. If you do decide to include the crew, it’s not actually super simple to paint - you are absolutely going to want to keep them separate and stick them in once they’re done. But, that said, it would have been nice to have the option to close the compartment, or to have different troopers available to differentiate them even further (I would have loved the option for an Ogryn rider!). 

Astra Militarum Hippogriff. Credit: NotThatHenryC

Hippogriff AFV

NotThatHenryC: The Hippogriff is built from a single sprue, containing an impressive amount of bits. There are several weapon options, a commander with a couple of head and arm options and some stowage.

I had the unexpected challenge of building my Hippogriff without instructions - a downside of splitting a box with other contributors. I found this relatively easy to do, though I’ve put a lot of plastic kits together so I suppose it’s something I’m fairly good at now. I was ready for issues like pieces that have to be poked through the upper hull before you stick it to the chassis, for example. Don’t be caught out by these.

I was impressed by the detail of the suspension. Each wheel has a pretty realistic-looking set of arms and springs, made out of just a couple of pieces. The front wheels also have a steering arm. A nice feature is that you can pivot the front wheels a bit where they connect to the suspension, making it look like the Hippogriff is turning. Personally I’d have been even happier if the rear wheels had this feature too, but it’s still nice to have.

I’ve my Hippogriff more or less as standard except for the commander. For this I used spare bits of DKoK Death Rider. The left arm took a little work, combining the forearm from a Death Rider with the shoulder and binocular hand from a Death Korps infantryman.

You could probably magnetise the Hippogriff’s guns but there aren’t any pre-drilled holes or anything. So far I’ve only painted the battlecannon and stubber because I like how that looks.

I was very happy with how the hippogriff went together. All the pieces fit nicely without needing too much cleanup. There aren’t all that many pieces but they manage to convey a lot of detail, with bits like the suspension and engine looking like they might actually work. The stowage is nice, particularly a piece moulded to fit over the texture on a hatch on the rear hull. I used all of it because I think a long-range patrol vehicle would be covered in this kind of stuff.

I painted my Hippogriff in a scheme I’ve used on some Solar Auxilia. It’s a relatively easy camo scheme because it has big patches of colour. I left the wheels off for painting until washes had been applied. The wheels and lower hull got a liberal amount of brown pigment applied so it looks suitably mucky. I may add some mud spatter and more on the wheels in future but wanted to leave the kit relatively clean for the review. I added a few DKoK transfers, which I think look pretty good.

I’m happy with this kit. To me it seems quite realistic, like it could actually exist. I’ve got a fair number of guard vehicles now and I’m considering making a proper army of them for 11th.



keewa: A 40k version of the classic British Saladin AFV? How cool! The Astra Militarum were missing a light armoured cavalry vehicle, so this little fella slots perfectly into their range of armour and really looks the part. The Hippogriff has a few options in terms of armament and cosmetic stowage; the main gun can be either a tank-style Vigilator cannon, a heavy lascannon, a Chiron gatling cannon, or a melta cannon, with a coaxial heavy stubber or meltagun. The top hatch can either be buttoned up or have a sergeant poking out the top - with a choice of two heads and either an arm pointing a pistol or waving. For the classic 40k vibe, it would’ve been nice to have an arm waving a sword instead of the (kind of nonsensical) pistol-brandishing option. Does a tank commander waving a sword make more sense? Certainly not, but it does look cooler. You also have the option to change the way the front wheels are facing, which is a little strange, but if you want to show your tank steering, well, you can do that.

I’ve seen a bit of criticism online that the Hippogriff is perhaps too realistic, since it’s a very close 40k version of a real 20th-century vehicle with very little sci-fi-ification. Those criticisms, that GW is displaying a lack of imagination, are valid - although I really love the little guy and don’t share them. The kit goes together very nicely, without too many annoying gaps or difficult parts to juggle - in common with GW’s usual practise, the turret doesn’t glue on and is movable and removable.   

Unlike Henry, I was caught out by the two attachment ring pieces that poke through the top of the chassis (although I actually had the instructions, for shame!), so I glued some jerry cans over where the holes were - I actually like how that looks, so I guess we can call that a happy accident! The rest of the stowage is what you’d ordinarily expect on a combat vehicle, there’s a large single piece that fits behind the turret with a monkey wrench, axe, various tarpaulins etc, and for stowage that goes just about anywhere you’ve the aforementioned Jerry Cans, crates, backpacks, axes, bolt-cutters, shovels, and a braided steel winch cable that fits onto the bonnet, you really can stuff your Hippogriff with everything a reconnaissance cavalry unit might need in the field - none of this is structurally necessary to the model, so it’s all optional. I really like this little fella, any Astra Militarum player is going to want a couple at least. 

Cadian Hippogriff AFV. Credit: Rich Nutter

Cronch: Keewa is bang on, this little guy is Saladin to the core, and if you know anyone whose first response was “wow cool, 40k version of the WWII German Puma!” then they are telling on themselves a little. It doesn’t even have the same number of wheels! In reality of course the little Hippogriff does take design cues from a variety of places, blending them to create a reasonably realistic armoured car fit for the Astra Militarum.

I really enjoyed putting this kit together, and it didn’t take long at all. Even some classic vehicle pitfalls were avoided, with the tyres being single piece and the mould line not crossing any recessed tread areas. All in all, a real joy.

Initially I started painting up the Hippogriff in a scheme I’d used previously on some Krieg models, retaining the kind of dusty blue with red and white markings. I didn’t have any spare Krieg bits, however, so as I started working on the commander and his highly-effective laspistol I decided to change things up a bit and make it a Cadian vehicle. I don’t have a Guard army, so as a one-off I didn’t worry too much about markings or regiments, adding transfers where they felt like they might be realistic. I particularly like the inclusion of some things like ID markings and license numbers on the transfer sheet!

As I finished the model off, I decided to lean a little into the military modelling side of things, and used a premixed enamel pigment product to run dust into the upper recesses and all over the tires. I really feel like this elevates the result a lot, and I’m super happy with how it came out!
