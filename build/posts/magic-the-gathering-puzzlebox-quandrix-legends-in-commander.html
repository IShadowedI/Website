Magic: The Gathering Puzzlebox – Quandrix Legends in Commander

April 28th, 2026 via Goonhammer
Simic gets a bit of a bad rap when it comes to Commander, as the culmination of the format's ethos, and—from a casual perspective—the best two-color pairing. Green gets you the acceleration & board-based win conditions that are a hallmark of the format, and Blue provides access to an expansive interaction suite, largely to both complement & protect what Green does for you. That's Simic, though, and in Secrets of Strixhaven there's not a Krasis to be seen; Blue & Green has another name, the school of math geeks known as Quandrix. Quandrix's identity between the two Strixhaven sets has been one of oddball scalers, jumping through hoops to grow massive tokens, all in service of pulling upon counter synergies. Secrets provides a fair few options for Blue & Green, so much so that there's more than I could cover in a single Commander Focus. So, today we're taking a look at a few options that aren't being blown up into a full primer, being Berta, Wise Extrapolator, Primo, the Unbounded, & Tam, Observant Sequencer.

This is part of a shortform Magic series where I’m covering rapid-fire concepts about Commander that I couldn’t cover in a full Commander Focus, so if you like what you see, certainly let us know!

To Combo with a Wooden Spoon

Credit: Wizards of the Coast

Berta is a card with some profoundly exciting characteristics, for someone who's a fan of silly interactions, and I'm lucky enough to count myself among that lot. Increment obviously is Quandrix's brand-new keyword in Secrets of Strixhaven, but the meat of Berta lies in her other two lines of text. Whenever she gets a +1/+1 counter, you get 1 mana of your choice—this is obviously amazing, and makes her a Birgi-esque new tool for Simic counter decks, but surprisingly I'm most hyped for that last ability. For {X} and tap, you get a Fractal with X +1/+1 counters. What's so cool about that? For starters, a lack of a Sorcery-speed restriction helps her play reactively, and allows you to hold up mana on opponents' turns for things like Counterspells. The better part is, however, there's no mandatory minimum payment for this ability. No "{X} can't be 0", just the ability to tap Berta and send a poor Fractal on a speedrun through life. She taps, and a token Creature enters & dies, simple as that. For the players who've been around for a while, this play pattern resembles one of the first real Commanders made specifically for the format: Marath, Will of the Wild from Commander 2013. Famously, Marath was first printed with no minimum for its X-cost ability, which was quickly errataed; Berta isn't quite so obviously broken, given she needs to tap, but when has that stopped us?

The obvious use case here is, unsurprisingly, Intruder Alarm[mtg_card][/mtg_card]. While this does allow you to produce infinite instantly-dead tokens, that alone doesn't win the game, so we need to find other outlets for death. Given Berta's other unbounded line of text, why not turn our eyes to Blade of the Bloodchief & Tarrian's Soulcleaver? There's actually a ton of effects which translate Berta's tap ability into a +1/+1 counter, which in turn is translated into mana...which we can in turn translate to an untap effect, by way of things like Freed from the Real or Pemmin's Aura. My pitch for Berta is to play her as token-y aristocrats, transposed onto the nebulous form of a Simic counters-matter deck—it's something we haven't seen before, really, and dodges the obvious path to play Berta as a fragile pure combo strategy.

El Primo Supremo

Credit: Wizards of the Coast

Primo is a Commander I wrote off initially, as can be seen in our review article, though in mulling it over I've warmed up to the very good boy. The thrust of Primo is combat, swinging in and pumping out larger & yet larger Fractals on top of a Commander that is himself a mana sink. Primo is a card you're expected to play fairly, and is conservatively costed to not get out of hand too fast. In terms of breaking him, however, the mana sink facet has clear use, with Blue & Green giving you access to redundancy for both Food Chain lines with Misthollow Griffin and a card I can't help but mention, Eternal Scourge, but he also gets large enough when paired with a new combo in SOS: Preparing Yavimaya Bloomsage ahead of schedule. This is done via Skycoach Waypoint, or the ability to give Channel Flash, and hey, in Blue we've got a wealth of ways to make that work. Leyline of Anticipation, Borne Upon a Wind, even High Fae Trickster, and a Channeled Primo is a very big boy. You can even flex those land tutors for Emergence Zone. I see the other component of the deck as similarly important though: Ways to swing with a 0/0 the turn he comes out. Slime Against Humanity is a reasonable path, and running with that, we can pull upon Blue's Artifact tutors to cycle through our Library with Altar of Dementia...which is also a win condition with a suitably large Primo. Or multiple, if we're Food Chain-ing out. There's also benefit of laundering your Creatures onto those of your opponents: Nanogene Conversion is a one-sided board wipe for any Creature without a +1/+1 counter. Finally, tie this in a neat bow with extra turn spells, as a means to instantly use the Fractal you've made after damage, and you've got a proper stew going. Savor the Moment is essentially an extra combat+Explore, after all. This take on Primo is very much the same as another Commander I enjoy, being Edea, Possessed Sorceress, hedging the power of your Commander's colors and a free combat trigger to eventually win via overwhelming aggression, seasoned with a modest combo. I may have just talked myself into covering him.

Finally, there's Tam, Observant Sequencer. I'm a fan of all these Prepare Legends, but Tam's a bit of a weak spot. She asks that you build Blue-Green Landfall, oh joy, how riveting. Statistically speaking, you either are or know an Aesi, Tyrant of Gyre Strait player, or one still hoping Tatyova is good enough in 2026, so what does Tam bring to the archetype? Much like Lluwen, Exchange Student, the benefits arrive in the form of Prepare as a mechanic itself (As an example, you can take a look at my list for him here). You can trigger multicolor-matters synergies like Verdant Eidolon or Tome of the Guildpact, things which care about the casting of Sorceries, or payoffs for slinging Spells from exile (e.g. Osgood, Operation Double). The pool of those is similarly potent when swapping Black for Blue, and you retain some of the strengths of cards like Fallaji Wayfarer (albeit not to the extent Lluwen abuses it). In short, if you want to play Simic Landfall, but have a contrarian streak, Tam might be just who you're looking for—I say that with love, as someone who undoubtedly meets that criteria.

A Class Above

The history of Blue-Green as a color pairing is one I've seen actively evolve during the course of my Magic career. It began, of course, with essentially no top decks back when 60-card was the norm, beyond the odd Blue-Green Madness, or Simic Evolve aggro. Slowly but surely, as design principles shifted towards Commander as a consideration, and indeed that players wanted to die with a full grip of options, archetypes like Simic Food were born, mistakes like Oko, Thief of Crowns were made; the funny bit is that this pair was always strong in Commander, because value generation and flexible disruption never goes out of style there. To that end, we've seen Simic as a pairing be a bit of a boogeyman, with things like Kinnan & Nadu be potent (even bannable). I make a point of this because Secrets has done a fantastic job of leaning into what these colors do, without making them paint-by-numbers, or too strong. There's a wealth of interesting options that do roughly what you'd expect, but in novel ways—in new combinations.

While I believe each of these options lends new opportunities to the color pairing, the elephant (or Elder Dragon) in the room remains: Quandrix, the Proof. I'll be covering her in a full article later this week, and I'll be so bold as to claim it's one of my best brews in a while. There's more to that Commander than just jamming Suspend pieces, trust me. What mechanic or Commander should I cover next on The Puzzlebox? This is a series built on rapid prototyping underutilized mechanics for Commander, and while I have a few ideas racing around my head, I'd love to hear more ideas from my readers!
